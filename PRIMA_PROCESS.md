# The Prima Process

### A Unified Brainstorming System for Original, Shippable Game Concepts

---

## How to Use This Document

This is the complete operational guide for running a Prima session. It contains everything you need — exercises, examples, templates, scoring rubrics, and decision rules — to go from a blank page to a documented, evaluated game concept in a single sitting.

Read it once end-to-end before your first session. After that, use the **Quick Reference Checklist** at the end as your session companion.

**Estimated session time:** 2–3 hours
**Minimum output:** 1 documented Concept Sheet, 10+ archived idea seeds

---

## Core Philosophy

A great game idea is not a stroke of genius — it is the collision of a **mechanic**, a **feeling**, and an **audience**, manufactured deliberately under **constraints**. Prima's job is to engineer those collisions repeatably.

Three rules govern every session:

1. **Constraints come first.** You define the box before you think inside it.
2. **Generation and judgment never overlap.** Produce volume in Phases 2–3. Apply filters in Phases 4–5. Mixing them kills the best ideas before they form.
3. **Every idea is captured.** Nothing is deleted. Today's reject is next month's breakthrough.

---

## Before You Begin

### Mindset Reset

Get out of consumer mode. Stop thinking "what game do I want to play?" and start asking "what experience do I want to create?"

**Exercise — The Blank Slate Audit (5 min):**
Write down the last 3 games you played. For each one, identify *one thing* that frustrated or bored you. These are not inspirations to copy — they are gaps in the market. Keep them visible during the session.

### Gather Research Inputs (Do This the Day Before)

Load your brain with raw material from *outside* games:

- Scan one non-gaming topic you know nothing about (deep sea biology, cold war espionage, actuarial science, competitive dog grooming — anything)
- Browse Steam's "New & Trending" for 10 minutes — note which tags appear on games with few reviews but high engagement
- Pull one visual reference (a painting, a photograph, a building) that creates a strong emotional reaction
- Review your **Spark File** — a running document where you dump interesting concepts year-round, not just during sessions

Do this the day *before* your session so your subconscious works overnight.

### Tools to Have Ready

- This document (or the Prima plugin)
- A writing surface: Notion, Obsidian, index cards, or a whiteboard
- Your Spark File
- A timer (every phase is time-boxed)

## Phase 1 — Anchor

**Time-box: 10 minutes**

Every session begins with constraints. You define three boundaries that every idea must fit inside. Nothing generated outside these boundaries survives.

### Choose Your Constraints

**1. Input Constraint** — What the player physically does:

| Example Constraints |
|---|
| Mouse only |
| One button |
| Touch only / no buttons |
| No UI — all information is diegetic |
| Player can only move, never attack |
| Only two inputs (e.g., left click + spacebar) |

**2. Scope Constraint** — How the game is bounded:

| Example Constraints |
|---|
| Entire game takes place in one scene/room |
| Session lasts exactly 7 minutes |
| Game world resets every 10 minutes |
| Grid-based — no free movement |
| No more than 3 entity types |
| Single core mechanic — no secondary systems |

**3. Production Window** — How long you have to ship:

| Window | What It Means |
|---|---|
| 3 weeks | Extreme scope discipline. One mechanic, one scene, AI-generated assets. |
| 4–5 weeks | Room for one secondary system or hand-crafted visual layer. |
| 6 weeks | Can support a progression arc or light narrative wrapper. |

### Write the Constraint Card

Capture your three constraints in a single block. This card stays visible for the entire session.

```
CONSTRAINT CARD
───────────────────────────
Input:      [e.g., Mouse only]
Scope:      [e.g., One scene, 20-minute sessions]
Window:     [e.g., 3 weeks]
───────────────────────────
```

**Phase 1 complete when:** You have a written constraint card with all three fields filled.

## Phase 2 — Generate

**Time-box: 45–60 minutes | Run 2–3 techniques**

Produce raw ideas at volume. Aim for at least 10 idea seeds. **Do not filter, evaluate, or discard anything during this phase.** Write down every idea, including the ones that feel stupid. Stupid ideas recombine into good ones.

Pick 2–3 techniques from the six below. You don't need to use all of them in every session — rotate across sessions to keep generation fresh.

---

### Technique 1 — Verb-First Design (15 min)

Games are about what the player *does*, not what they *are*. Start with an action, not a genre.

**Exercise:**
1. Write 10 verbs that have nothing to do with combat. Avoid "jump," "shoot," "run," "fight," "collect."
   - *Starter list if stuck: negotiate, decay, translate, synchronize, misremember, inherit, contaminate, migrate, repair, impersonate, sling, inflate, magnetize, freeze, rewind, eavesdrop, tether, erode, splice, calibrate*
2. Pick 2 verbs at random
3. Combine them into a single core mechanic
4. Ask: "What genre would make this verb combination feel most powerful?"
5. Write the idea as one sentence

**Example:**
> *Misremember + Repair* → A puzzle game where you reconstruct fragmented memories, but your reconstructions are imperfect and change the world around you. Genre: narrative puzzle / unreliable narrator.

**Constraint check:** Does this idea work within your input and scope constraints? If yes, keep it. If no, keep it anyway — it's Phase 2. But mark it with a warning.

---

### Technique 2 — Genre Collision (10 min)

Take two genres that don't belong together. The friction between them *is* the idea.

**Exercise:**
1. Draw one genre from each column (randomly or by gut):

| Column A (Core Genre) | Column B (Unexpected Layer) |
|---|---|
| Farming Sim | Tactical Espionage |
| Battle Royale | Cozy Crafting |
| Horror Survival | City Builder |
| Rhythm Game | Immersive Sim |
| Tower Defense | Romance / Dating |
| Puzzle Platformer | Documentary / Journalism |
| Idle / Clicker | Courtroom Drama |
| Roguelike | Interior Design |

2. Ask: "What's the *one rule* that makes this hybrid make sense?"
3. Write the idea as one sentence

**Example:**
> *Horror Survival + City Builder* → You manage a besieged medieval town during an endless supernatural siege. Resource management by day, desperate survival by night. Players optimize for resilience, not growth.

---

### Technique 3 — Constraint Stack (15 min)

Layer additional constraints on top of your Phase 1 Anchor and work backward to find the game that could exist under all of them.

**Exercise:**
1. Assign one constraint from each category (roll a die, draw a card, or pick the one that intrigues you most):

   **Input constraints:**
   - The player can only use one button
   - No UI at all
   - Player can only move, never directly act

   **Narrative constraints:**
   - The protagonist has already lost
   - The villain is correct
   - There is no ending — the game is a permanent state
   - The player doesn't know the goal

   **Scope constraints:**
   - The entire game takes place in one room
   - The game lasts exactly 7 minutes
   - There are only 3 objects in the world

2. Stack these on top of your Phase 1 constraints
3. Ask: "Given *all* of these constraints, what game could work?"
4. Write the idea as one sentence

**Example:**
> Constraints: *One button / The villain is correct / 7 minutes exactly* → A one-button narrative game where you play as the antagonist in the final moments of a story you've already lost. The player's choices recontextualize everything — the "hero" was wrong all along.

---

### Technique 4 — What-If Inversion (10 min)

Take rules that every game in a genre obeys. Flip them.

**Exercise:**
1. Pick a genre you know well
2. Write 5 "sacred cow" rules that nearly every game in that genre follows
3. Invert each one — write the opposite as a premise

**Example — Roguelike inversions:**
- "Death is permanent" → *What if death made you stronger, but the world weaker?*
- "Runs start from zero" → *What if every run starts from a random mid-game state?*
- "You play one character" → *What if you play as the dungeon itself?*
- "Items are randomly generated" → *What if every item is hand-placed, but the player is randomized?*
- "The goal is to go deeper" → *What if the goal is to escape upward from the bottom?*

Each inversion is a raw idea seed. Don't explain it yet — just capture it.

---

### Technique 5 — Emotional Target (15 min)

Most games start with a mechanic. This technique starts with a feeling.

**Exercise:**
1. Choose one target emotion that games rarely deliver well:
   > *Peaceful competence / Productive guilt / Nostalgic dread / Earned loneliness / Reluctant power / Quiet urgency / Tender defiance / Bittersweet efficiency*

2. Answer three questions:
   - When in real life does someone feel this way?
   - What kind of *decision* creates this feeling?
   - What mechanic could force that decision repeatedly?

3. Write the idea as one sentence

**Example:**
> Target: *Reluctant power* → Real life: a parent overriding a child's decision for their own good. Decision: choosing how much autonomy to give an NPC who trusts you completely. Mechanic: a management game where giving NPCs freedom improves their happiness but reduces your control — and the story requires you to need that control.

---

### Technique 6 — Alien Anthropologist (10 min)

Describe a mundane human activity to someone with zero cultural context. The misunderstanding becomes a game premise.

**Exercise:**
1. Pick a mundane activity: doing taxes, airport security, a job interview, parallel parking, a dinner party, grocery shopping, a dentist appointment
2. Describe it using only mechanical terms — inputs, outputs, win states, fail states. Strip all human meaning.
3. Ask: what game lives in this pure logic?

**Example:**
> Airport security described mechanically: *Players sort objects into categories under time pressure while adversarial agents attempt to smuggle ambiguous items past your judgment.* → A precision sorting game with social deduction and escalating ambiguity.

---

### Capture Your Seeds

After running 2–3 techniques, you should have at least 10 raw ideas. Write them all in a single list. Each seed is one sentence. No scores, no rankings, no commentary.

```
RAW IDEA SEEDS — Session [date]
─────────────────────────────────
1. [idea]
2. [idea]
3. [idea]
...
```

**Phase 2 complete when:** You have 10+ idea seeds written down with no filtering applied.

## Phase 3 — Refine

**Time-box: 20–30 minutes**

Read through your seed list. Select the top 3–5 that interest you most. Don't overthink the selection — gut feel is fine here because this phase will pressure-test them.

Each selected idea goes through two passes. Ideas that fail either pass are cut.

### Pass 1 — Mechanical Friction (10–15 min)

A game without friction is a toy. Every core verb needs a cost, a limit, or a trade-off that makes it difficult.

**Exercise — The "Yes, But..." Rule:**

For each idea, write one sentence in this format:
> *Yes, you can [core verb], **but** [cost / limit / trade-off].*

The friction must create a recurring decision — something the player weighs every time they act, not just once.

**Examples:**
- *Yes, you can **sling** debris at enemies, but every shot costs 10% of your ship's fuel.*
- *Yes, you can **tether** flowers to spread your garden, but the soil has limited vitality that drains with each connection.*
- *Yes, you can **rewind** time, but each rewind ages your character and the world remembers what you undid.*

**Cut test:** If you can't write a friction rule that feels like it creates real tension, the mechanic is too shallow. Remove the idea from the refinement pool and return it to the seed archive.

### Pass 2 — Thematic Subversion (10–15 min)

Take each surviving idea and apply the *least* obvious theme. The goal is to prevent genre cliches from flattening your originality.

**Exercise — The Contrast Test:**

| If the Mechanic Feels... | Apply a Theme That Feels... |
|---|---|
| High-stress (timers, pressure, urgency) | Zen (gardening, painting, tea ceremony, stargazing) |
| Slow and methodical (planning, building, sorting) | High-octane (cyberpunk, space combat, heist, racing) |
| Dark or grim (decay, loss, survival) | Playful or absurd (cartoon, food, children's toys) |
| Wholesome or cozy (nurture, grow, connect) | Unsettling or eerie (liminal spaces, uncanny valley, bureaucracy) |

Write the subverted theme next to each idea.

**Cut test:** If the "obvious" theme is the only one that works — if the idea *requires* its default genre skin to make sense — it's not distinctive enough. Return it to the seed archive.

### Write the Refined Cards

Each surviving idea (typically 2–4) gets a refined card:

```
REFINED IDEA CARD
───────────────────────────
Seed:       [Original one-sentence idea]
Mechanic:   [Core verb]
Friction:   [The "Yes, but..." rule]
Theme:      [The subverted skin]
Constraint: [Still fits Phase 1 anchor? Y/N]
───────────────────────────
```

**Phase 3 complete when:** You have 2–4 refined idea cards with mechanic, friction rule, and subverted theme. Ideas that failed either pass are back in the seed archive.

## Phase 4 — Expand

**Time-box: 20–30 minutes per idea | Apply to your refined cards**

A refined idea is a seed with roots. Expansion determines whether it can grow into a full concept. You are not designing the game yet — you are testing whether the idea can sustain a player experience.

Work through all five layers in order for each refined idea. **Do not skip to Layer 3 before finishing Layer 1.**

### Layer 1 — The Core Promise

Write one sentence using this exact structure:

> *"This is a game where you [core verb] in order to [player goal], and it feels like [target emotion]."*

If you can't complete this sentence, the idea doesn't have a clear identity yet. Go back to the refined card and sharpen the mechanic or theme before continuing.

**Example:**
> "This is a game where you **tether** root networks in order to **reclaim a dying ecosystem**, and it feels like **quiet urgency**."

### Layer 2 — The Signature Moment

Describe the single most memorable moment a player could have with this game. One paragraph. Be specific — name the objects, the choice, the consequence.

This is the moment a player would describe to a friend. If you can't picture it clearly, the idea isn't ready for expansion. Send it back to the seed archive.

**Example:**
> You've spent ten minutes carefully connecting three root clusters to a water source. The soil vitality meter is low. A new seed appears at the far edge of the grid — it's rare, it would complete a synergy chain that doubles your growth rate. But tethering to it means routing through depleted soil. You'll drain the entire eastern network to reach it. Do you sacrifice established growth for a chance at exponential return, or play it safe and lose the rare seed forever?

### Layer 3 — The Loop

Define what the player does at three time scales. If any loop is empty, that's a design problem — it means there's a dead zone in the experience.

| Time Scale | Question | Your Answer |
|---|---|---|
| **3 minutes** (micro) | What is the player doing moment-to-moment? | |
| **30 minutes** (meso) | What goal or cycle is the player working toward? | |
| **Full session** (macro) | What arc or progression defines the entire play session? | |

**Example:**
> - *3 min:* Place tethers between nodes, manage soil vitality, decide which connections to maintain or sever
> - *30 min:* Complete synergy chains to unlock new root types, manage the spread/sustain trade-off across the grid
> - *Session:* Reclaim a full biome from decay, face a final drain event that tests whether your network can survive

### Layer 4 — The Hook

Finish this sentence:

> *"No other game does [X] because..."*

If you can't finish it, the concept doesn't have enough differentiation. Iterate on the mechanic, the friction, or the theme — or cut the idea.

**Example:**
> "No other game makes **ecosystem management feel like circuit design** because no other game treats root networks as literal electrical connections with vitality as current."

### Layer 5 — The Audience Gut-Check

Name 3 real, existing games that your target player already loves. Then ask: would those players be **surprised**, **intrigued**, or **confused** by your concept?

- **Surprise + Intrigue** = strong signal. You're offering something adjacent to what they already enjoy, but genuinely new.
- **Confusion** = the pitch is unclear. The idea might be fine — but the way you're describing it needs work.
- **Indifference** = wrong audience, or the hook isn't landing. Revisit Layer 4.

**Example:**
> Target players love: *Islanders* (minimal, spatial puzzle), *Opus Magnum* (optimization satisfaction), *A Short Hike* (cozy exploration with stakes). These players would be **intrigued** — the spatial puzzle is familiar, the electrical metaphor is novel, the ecological stakes add emotional weight they don't get from pure optimization games.

### Write the Expanded Concept

```
EXPANDED CONCEPT — [Working Title]
──────────────────────────────────────
Core Promise:     [Layer 1 sentence]
Signature Moment: [Layer 2 paragraph]
Loop:
  3 min:          [Micro loop]
  30 min:         [Meso loop]
  Session:        [Macro loop]
Hook:             [Layer 4 sentence]
Audience:         [3 games + reaction prediction]
──────────────────────────────────────
```

**Phase 4 complete when:** You have 1–3 expanded concepts with all five layers filled. Ideas that couldn't fill all layers are returned to the seed archive or sent back to Refine.

## Phase 5 — Evaluate

**Time-box: 20–30 minutes**

Every expanded concept is now scored on two independent axes. Neither axis alone is sufficient — a brilliant idea that can't be built is useless, and a buildable idea that isn't interesting isn't worth the production cycle.

### Axis 1 — CRISP Score

Rate each concept 1–5 on five dimensions. Be honest. Overscoring here costs you weeks of production time later.

| Dimension | 1 (Weak) | 3 (Solid) | 5 (Exceptional) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **C**reativity | "This exists already" | "Fresh angle on a known genre" | "I've never seen anything like this" |
| **R**esonance | "Players won't feel much" | "There's a clear emotional hook" | "This will stay with players after they stop" |
| **I**dentity | "Generic look and feel" | "Distinctive but familiar" | "Unmistakable — you'd know it from a screenshot" |
| **S**cope | "Needs a team and 6+ months" | "Tight but achievable solo" | "Could ship in under 3 weeks" |
| **P**ull | "I wouldn't play this" | "I'd try it" | "I'd play this tonight and tell someone about it" |

**Total: ___/25**

#### Scoring Traps to Avoid

- **The Pivot Trap:** Don't change the idea during scoring to make it score higher. Evaluate what's there, not what it could become.
- **The Complexity Cope:** Don't assume a weak concept becomes strong if you add more systems. Complexity doesn't create meaning.
- **The Audience Fantasy:** Don't design for "gamers" as a monolith. If your Pull score relies on a vague audience, it's a 1.
- **The Nostalgia Bias:** Ask whether you love the *player feeling* or the *specific execution of a game you already played*. If it's the latter, you're cloning, not creating.

### Axis 2 — Viability Audit

Four pass/fail gates. Be ruthless. A concept that fails even one gate has a structural problem that excitement won't fix.

| Gate | The Test | Pass / Fail |
|---|---|---|
| **One-Scene Rule** | Can the entire core loop happen in a single scene or level? If it needs multiple distinct environments to work, it's too complex for the production window. | |
| **AI-Asset Fit** | Can AI generate the necessary assets (2D sprites, textures, basic music, sound effects) effectively for this theme? If the art style requires hand-drawn precision or the audio needs live recording, the window will break. | |
| **Math Check** | Can the core "fun" be expressed as a simple formula? *Example: `Growth = (Synergy * Nutrients) / Distance`*. If you can't write the formula, the systems design is too complex to implement in time. | |
| **Feature Sizing** | List every feature the concept needs to be fun (not polished — fun). Tag each S / M / L / XL. If there are more than 3 XL features, the scope is out of control. | |

### Route the Concept

| CRISP Score | Viability | Route | What Happens Next |
|---|---|---|---|
| 18–25 | All gates pass | **Active** | Prototype immediately. This is your next game. |
| 12–17 | All gates pass | **Greenhouse** | Promising but not ready. Revisit next month or when constraints change. |
| 18–25 | 1+ gates fail | **Greenhouse** | Strong concept with a feasibility problem. Solve the failing gate or wait for better tooling. |
| 12–17 | 1+ gates fail | **Greenhouse** | Park it. May recombine with future ideas. |
| Under 12 | Any | **Archive** | Preserve the seed. Don't delete it. It may contribute to a future concept. |

### Write the Evaluation Card

```
EVALUATION — [Working Title]
──────────────────────────────────────
CRISP:
  C: [1-5]  R: [1-5]  I: [1-5]  S: [1-5]  P: [1-5]
  Total: [X/25]

Viability:
  One-Scene:    [Pass/Fail]
  AI-Asset Fit: [Pass/Fail]
  Math Check:   [Pass/Fail — include formula if passing]
  Feature Size: [Pass/Fail — list XL features if any]

Route: [Active / Greenhouse / Archive]
──────────────────────────────────────
```

**Phase 5 complete when:** Every expanded concept has a CRISP score, viability audit results, and a routing decision.

## Phase 6 — Document

**Time-box: 15–20 minutes per concept**

Every concept routed to **Active** or **Greenhouse** produces a standardized Concept Sheet. This locks in what you actually decided — no silent mutation, no scope drift, no lost insight.

### Filing

Every Concept Sheet is stored in one of three buckets:

- **Active** — Concepts in development or ready for immediate prototyping. These are your next games.
- **Greenhouse** — Concepts that scored well but have an unresolved feasibility issue or aren't the right fit for this cycle. Review monthly.
- **Archive** — Ideas that didn't score high enough to develop, but are preserved for recombination. Never delete these.

All raw idea seeds from Phase 2 that didn't make it through Refinement are also saved in the Archive. They cost nothing to keep and may become valuable under different constraints.

**Phase 6 complete when:** Every Active and Greenhouse concept has a written Concept Sheet filed in the correct bucket. All remaining seeds are archived.

---

## The Compounding Library

Prima's value multiplies over time. After 12 sessions across a year, you have:

- **120+ archived idea seeds** — raw material for future generation phases
- **24–36 expanded concepts** — a catalog of scored, compared ideas
- **12+ Concept Sheets** — a portfolio of production-ready or near-ready game concepts

Each new session can draw on this library. During Phase 2 (Generate), scan the Archive for seeds that might work under your new constraint card. During Phase 3 (Refine), check whether a Greenhouse concept's failing viability gate has been resolved by new tools or skills. During Phase 5 (Evaluate), compare new concepts against your existing catalog to spot patterns in what scores well for you.

**Monthly review:** Spend 30 minutes reviewing all three buckets. Move concepts between buckets as circumstances change. Promote Greenhouse concepts whose blockers are resolved. Demote Active concepts that you've decided not to pursue. But never delete.
